Almost every morning I walk through central park. I walk around the reservoir and notice the creatures around me.

Milkweed is my favorite. I look for them as I walk. Like an old friend, they're familiar and I look forward to greeting them. We've come to know each other over years, thorugh various life stages.

They start as green stalks with pink blossoms. Monarch caterpillars eat their leaves; as do at least ten other species who depend on them exclusively.

Sometimes I see beetles spooning in the sunshine.

Or colonies of aphids beneath a single leaf.

Or these guys...butt-to-butt.

When their flowers seed, they become these smooth pods at first...

When you pick them off, the plant secretes it's defensive milk, which it uses to ward off predators. It's surprisingly sticky. It gives monarch caterpillars chemicals they need to taste gross to birds (a great defense mechanism). It's also the reason behind milkweed's name.

The pods grow in groups on mature plants. I'm always impressed by their mass and their whimsy â€” moonshaped, dinosaurs.

The plants themselves grow in groups. You'll find milkweeds abound in parks, abandoned fields, or empty lots.

Over time, the pods start to develop a fuzz and protrusions...

They become increasingly reminiscent of stegosaurs...

At this time, the seeds inside look like albino feathers on layered, white, pulpy fish scales. The pod itself is spongy.

In the fall, inside and outside, they darken. The seeds' "feathers" become softer and fluffier.

The pods open, and let their seeds fly away to become milkweed plants of their own. Their "feathers" carry them through the air.

By winter, everyone has flown away.

I see milkweed shells as I run. Dessicated sculptures.

Watching them fall apart is also beautiful.

Hi!

Like finding sea shells washed ashore,

There are gifts everywhere.
